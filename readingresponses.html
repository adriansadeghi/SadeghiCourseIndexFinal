My Response

In the first article author John-Patrick Hartnett expresses his concern about the duopoly that both Apple and Adobe hold over the graphic design industry. Hartnett is in favour of more accesible hardware and software which is evident
through his explanation that through seeking efficiency designers have allowed themselves to be held from their tools at a distance. Through reading this article it is fairly evident that Adobe have simplified workflows across their
applications, as well as integrating different digital features such as cloud storage in order to create a premium experience which users will break down the pay wall for. In the second article author Gavin Mueller delves into the politics
surrounding open-source software. Mueller opens his ideas through explaining that the true roots of personal computing are synonymous with open-source, that "cracking things open" and sharing what you learned as well as sharing
softwares themselves was a common trait within the community of personal computing. Mueller goes on to reflect on a statement made by Bill Gates in 1976 which entailed his awareness of software piracy, as well as his idea that
software piracy would result in nothing but a decrease in programmers willing to write code for software. It turned out that the majority of those involved with personal computing would disagree, seeing as their first times working with
code was likely studying and reverse-engineering softwares that had been shared with them. It is evident that Mueller is in the favour of open-source software as a method for sharing knowledge and improving software code on a
general basis. The Adobe suite provides a smooth workflow across apps at the cost of a monthly subscription, and as Hartnett explained, in a sense Adobe is always keeping designers tools at distance thus preventing greater influence.
It's evident in Mueller's text that through learning how each tools is actually founded and created, you can better understand the extremeties of which you can push that tool to. My connection is that if the Adobe suite were open-source
and everyone could crack open the code and learn the actual boundaries of each tool, not only would more people be able to create amazing things, but these people would no longer be relying on corporate entities. Aside from the
mastery of digital & physical tools, I believe the most integral part of the graphic design practice is to actually interpret everything alongside its potential purpose in the circuit of creation. What I am explaining is not to only look at things
for their potential use, but to acknowledge it. As a designer I believe a extremely important trait is to be able to look at what you create as an outsider; someone on the street just glancing at your work. In addition, it is also extremely
important to understand the different ways that what your looking at can be interpreted. Funny enough, I also believe this to be a somewhat open-source view on things. To look at a chair but not label it as one; rather a collection of wood
or whatever the material may be. To simply allow yourself to be a vessel for interpretation.

I believe Lialina's definition of the universal user to be extremely important in understanding Schwulst's argument between individual websites and platforms. Lialina defines the universal user as someone who is often in a rush since
using your website is not always their main priority. The universal user is a general umbrella term which encapsulates the average user and their habits. This can be directly related to Schwulst's argument advocating for individual sites

My Response

A major theme that runs throughout Steyerl’s essay is that of surveillance. In your own words, define surveillance and discuss how the notion of a “vertical” or aerial perspective lends itself to a culture of surveillance according to Steyerl’s
thought experiment. Surveillance as Hito Steyerl explains in his essay is understood as a trait of visuality that refers to a vertical, aerial view which is often captured by the likes of surveillance technologies such as Google maps, drones,
and various other tracking and targeting devices. This kind of visuality is modern in nature, seeing as Steyerl even states that this view is considered a "God's-eye view" (Steyerl). For a long time, humans have developed artistic styles
which innovate past the use of linear perspective, however the influence of modern technologies have enabled surveillance perspectives on a large scale. This modern visuality has great potential to lend itself useful to humanity moving
forward. For example, this new perspective may allow humans to map the vertical axis of territories worldwide more accurately. Unfortunately, while the access to this new visuality is revolutionary and provides a new perspective for humans
to consider the world through visually, there is still a dilemma with surveillance technologies. The greater areas of the world are captured through cameras which have been mounted onto boats and planes such as the google maps camera
would be attached to its respective car which drives around capturing its surroundings. While this is effective in covering large amounts of land and is crucial in the process of making something like Google Earth work so effectively, it falls
short in the sense that there is a serious lack of detail when looking in closer.
